What Is a DD214 Form?

Your military discharge papers are officially known as DD Form 214 (downloadable form). This “Certificate of Release or Discharge from Active Duty” is issued when you separate from active service. Every branch uses this same form.

In a nutshell: Your DD214 is your service “root credential.” It’s the proof of your military time, the nature of your discharge, what you did, and what you earned. Many benefits simply cannot be unlocked without it.

What Is a Reserve DD214‑1?

Reserve and Guard members now often receive a companion document, DD214‑1 (downloadable reference), the “Certificate of Uniformed Service, Reserve Component Addendum.” It summarizes active and inactive service and retirement points.

Active‑duty members usually get one DD214 for a continuous period of service. Reservists, however, can accumulate multiple DD214s over a career — one for each activation and release — plus the DD214‑1 as an overall summary.

Key Data the DD214 May Contain

Service Timeline

  • Active‑duty entry date
  • Service length and total creditable service
  • Place of entry and last duty station
  • Foreign service and deployments

Identity & Status

  • Rank/grade at separation
  • Component and branch
  • Military occupational specialty (MOS/AFSC/Rating)
  • Character of service (Honorable, etc.)

Training & Awards

  • Military education and courses completed
  • Decorations, medals, badges
  • Citations and campaign awards

Separation Codes

  • Type of separation
  • Separation authority and narrative reason
  • Reenlistment / separation codes

How to Get Your DD214

There are several official paths to retrieve your DD214. The two primary online channels are eVetRecs and milConnect, plus the classic Standard Form 180 (SF‑180) (downloadable form) for mail/fax requests.

Online Requests

eVetRecs (National Archives):
Official portal for requesting your DD214 and other records.

milConnect (DoD):
DoD’s online portal for accessing and updating various service and benefits information.

Mail & Fax via SF‑180

You can submit a Standard Form 180 (downloadable form) to the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC) by mail or fax.

National Personnel Records Center (NPRC)

1 Archives Drive
St. Louis, MO 63138

Fax for SF‑180 requests: 📞 314‑801‑9195

Fastest “Emergency” Path

  1. Submit an online request and select the “Emergency Request” option when appropriate.
  2. Or fax a clearly marked “Emergency” SF‑180 (e.g., for medical urgency, funeral honors timelines).
  3. For status questions, call NPRC customer service: 📞 314‑801‑0800
Processing Time Notice: Routine requests can take weeks to months. Emergency flags are reserved for time‑sensitive needs (life‑and‑death, funeral timing, homelessness, etc.).

Why Your DD214 Matters

This separation document is the verifier that many systems key off of. VA, DoD, state agencies, employers, and veteran organizations often require a DD214 to confirm your service and discharge status.

Think of it like your “master token.” Once your DD214 is verified, many other doors — health care, compensation, GI Bill, loans, and more — can open with far less friction.

Common Benefits That Need a DD214

Housing & Loans

  • VA home loan eligibility
  • Some property tax reductions
  • Certain state housing programs

Employment & Preference

  • Federal hiring preference
  • Verification for veteran‑only positions
  • Service‑based background verification

Education & Training

  • GI Bill benefits
  • State tuition waivers/discounts
  • Apprenticeship/credential programs

Health & Compensation

  • Enrollment in VA health care
  • Disability compensation
  • Certain insurance programs

Services & Honors

  • Burial benefits and honors
  • Headstone/marker eligibility
  • Veteran organization memberships

Funeral Honors

Your surviving spouse or family will also need your DD214 to arrange military funeral honors: folding and presentation of the flag, and the playing of taps, typically coordinated through the funeral home and DoD/VA channels.

Corrections & DD215 Updates

If there’s an error or missing information on your DD214, you can request an official correction. Approved changes are issued on a DD215 (downloadable form) and should always be stored together with the original DD214.

Keep them paired. Your DD214 and any DD215s together form your complete corrected service record. When you provide copies, include both.

Typical Corrections

  • Missing or incorrect awards and decorations
  • Typos in name, dates, or service periods
  • Updates to foreign service or duty stations
  • Corrections to military education entries

Requesting a Correction

The process varies by branch and by type of correction. For new awards or upgrades to the character of service, you may need to go through a Board for Correction of Military Records (BCMR/BCNR). For straightforward typos, a simpler records‑update process may be enough.

Privacy & Security: Protecting Your DD214

High‑value target for identity theft. Your DD214 contains full identity data plus service details. In the wrong hands, it can be used to open lines of credit, impersonate you with veteran‑serving organizations, or socially engineer your family.

Practical Protection Moves

  • Store originals in a fire‑resistant, locked container.
  • Use high‑quality copies for most verifications; only show originals when absolutely required.
  • Keep digital scans encrypted and backed up in a secure location.
  • Be cautious about emailing PDFs; if you must, use encryption or secure portals.

Should You File at a County Courthouse?

Some counties allow veterans to record a copy of their DD214. While this can be convenient, it can also increase exposure if the record becomes public or is not tightly controlled. Many jurisdictions now restrict access, but policies vary.

Action step: Before recording your DD214, verify how your county protects that record, who can request copies, and whether redaction is available.

Replacing Lost or Damaged Military Records

If your DD214 or related documents were lost, destroyed, or never received, you can rebuild your packet primarily through the National Archives and your service branch.

Primary Contact: NPRC

Cost & Scams

  • You are entitled to your own military records at no cost from the government.
  • Third‑party services may charge a fee to “help,” but they cannot make NPRC work faster than official channels.
  • Be wary of any site that looks unofficial or demands large payment up front.
